Introduction. The morning when I was not working

On Wednesday at 7:40, I opened my laptop and read a report about work I had not done.

Overnight, my agent closed four questions in the research I had been running for three weeks. It found pricing for two competitors, checked it against founder interviews, marked one of my hypotheses as “not supported — here is why,” and left me three questions the machine has no right to answer: they require a human decision. At the bottom — a plan for the next night.

I had not written it a prompt the evening before. I had not talked to it for two days.

This book is about the distance between two pictures. The first is familiar to everyone: you sit in front of a chat, write a request, get an answer, write the next one, correct, clarify, get tired. AI helps, but you remain the dispatcher of every step. The second picture is the one above: you set a mission, go live your life, and the work continues.

The distance between them is not “a better model” or “a cleverer prompt.” It is a different way to organize work.

Not “AI will do everything for you,” but “you will learn to build a system in which AI can carry a meaningful part of complex work, while you keep direction, criteria, and responsibility.”

Five laws of the core

  1. A mission without a definition of done never ends.
  2. The context window is not memory. It is a draft in the wind.
  3. The appearance of progress is more dangerous than stopping.
  4. A mission is held by external state, not by the model's memory.
  5. A one-off mission is heroics. A mission that leaves tools behind is a system.

Chapter 3. Mission: work that has an end

On Thursday, a client wrote to me. Two lines: “We are thinking of entering AI tutors for university entrance exams. Look at the market — is it worth it? You have a month.”

Letters like that used to mean three weeks of my life. Now I had an agent — and the temptation to write “do a market analysis.” I did, out of curiosity. I got an essay on the topic of analysis. Then I spent forty minutes defining the mission.

A mission is built from seven details. Skip any one — and the engine either will not start, or it will drive somewhere else:

  • Outcome — what is in the folder at the end
  • Boundaries — what is in scope and what is not
  • Sources — what you may rely on
  • Assumptions — what is taken as given
  • Format — length and structure
  • Reporting rhythm — how to catch drift early
  • Definition of done — when it is “ready”; a good criterion forbids something
A mission without a definition of done is not a mission. It is a subscription to endless text.
